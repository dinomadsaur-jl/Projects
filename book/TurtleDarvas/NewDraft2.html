Part One – Setting the Stage

Chapter 1 – What is a Trend? (The Ball on the Hill)

A trend is simply a persistent move in one direction. Imagine a ball on a hill: if you give it a little push, it rolls and keeps rolling until something stops it. Prices work the same way. When a stock starts moving up, it often continues because more people jump in. That’s momentum.

Kid analogy: You and your friends all want the same video game. As soon as a few buy it, others see it and want it too. The popularity keeps growing. That’s an uptrend.

There are three types: uptrend (higher highs), downtrend (lower lows), and sideways (range). We only care about uptrends, because that’s where we make money.

Chapter 2 – Why Most People Lose (The Lemonade Stand Mistake)

Imagine you open a lemonade stand. On a hot day, you sell 50 cups. You get excited and buy tons of lemons. Then it rains for three days. You’re stuck with lemons and lose money. Beginners do the same: they bet big after a win and get wiped out by a small loss. They have no plan.

The three main reasons beginners lose: 1) No system, 2) No risk control, 3) Emotions (greed and fear). This book fixes all three.

Part Two – The Legends

Chapter 3 – The Turtle Experiment: Ordinary People, Extraordinary Results

Chicago, early 1980s. Two famous traders, Richard Dennis and Bill Eckhardt, argue: Can anyone learn to trade, or is it a natural gift? To settle it, they place an ad: “Wanted: Trainee traders. No experience needed.” They pick 23 people — a varied group including a security guard, a pianist, and a student.

They teach them a simple trend-following system for two weeks. Then they give them real money. Over the next four years, the group earns over $100 million for their teachers. They become legends known as the Turtle Traders.

The Turtle rules were simple: breakouts, volatility-based position sizing, and letting winners run. They never predicted — they reacted.

Chapter 4 – Nicolas Darvas: The Dancer Who Traded From His Hotel

1950s. Nicolas Darvas was a professional dancer, touring the world. He had no finance background. But he noticed something: stocks that were moving sideways then broke to new highs often soared. He started buying those breakouts using a simple method: draw a box around the trading range. If price broke above, he bought. He made millions while dancing on stage.

Darvas later wrote “How I Made $2,000,000 in the Stock Market.” His method: find boxes, wait for breakouts, and ride the trend.

Chapter 5 – How They Both Thought About Trends

Both Turtles and Darvas understood that you can’t predict when a big trend will start. But you can recognize when it’s happening. They waited for price to “prove itself” (breakout) and then held on with a trailing stop. No predictions, just reaction.

Part Three – Building Your Turtle-Darvas System

Chapter 6 – The Box: Darvas Made Simple

A Darvas box is a period where price bounces between a clear top and bottom. Look for at least three touches at the top (resistance) and two at the bottom (support). The longer the box, the more significant the breakout.

Box rules:
Top = highest price in the consolidation
Bottom = lowest price in the consolidation
Stock XYZ: prices 48, 49, 50, 49, 48, 50, 48. Top = 50, Bottom = 48. That’s your box.

Chapter 7 – Volatility and N (The Turtle’s Secret Weapon)

The Turtles used N to measure volatility. For beginners: N is simply the average daily movement over 20 days (ATR). If N = $2, the stock typically moves $2 per day.

N = 20-day Average True Range (ATR)

Why N matters: it tells you how far to place your stop so normal noise doesn’t kick you out.

Chapter 8 – Risk First: The 1% Rule

This is the golden rule: never risk more than 1% of your account on a single trade. If you have $10,000, your risk per trade = $100. This ensures you survive losing streaks.

Chapter 9 – Position Sizing: How Many Shares?

Position size = (1% of account) ÷ (stop distance). Stop distance = 2 × N. Example: $10,000 account, N=$2, stop distance = $4. Risk $100 → $100/$4 = 25 shares.

Units = Account × 0.01 / (2 × N)

Chapter 10 – Entries: Buying the Breakout

Once a Darvas box is identified, wait for a daily close above the top. Enter next day at market. Volume should ideally be above average.

Don’t chase if it’s already 5% above the box — patience. Another box will form.

Chapter 11 – Let Winners Run: The Trailing Stop (Your Best Friend)

This is the most critical part of the book. Beginners sell too early because they’re scared. We use a trailing stop to let profits run. After entry, we have an initial stop at 2×N below entry. Once price moves up 2×N, we move stop to breakeven. Then we trail it: stop = highest price since entry minus 2×N. Or use the Darvas method: as new boxes form, raise stop to the bottom of the new box.

Think of a cat on a hot tin roof: you keep moving the safety net higher as the cat climbs. If it falls, it lands on the net.

Chapter 12 – Pyramiding: Adding Fuel to the Fire

The Turtles added to winning positions. Every 0.5N move above entry, add another unit. But each new unit has its own stop. This way you’re bigger in the strongest trends.

Chapter 13 – Exit Strategies: When to Say Goodbye

Your trailing stop is your exit. If price falls below your trailing stop (2×N from peak, or breaks a Darvas box floor), you’re out. No hesitation. Small losses are fine; big winners pay.

Part Five – The Beginner’s Action Plan

Chapter 18 – Your Step-by-Step Checklist

  1. Open a small practice account (or use a simulator).
  2. Pick 10 liquid stocks or ETFs.
  3. Draw boxes on weekly charts. Mark tops and bottoms.
  4. Calculate N using 20-day ATR.
  5. When price closes above box top, enter next day.
  6. Set initial stop at 2×N below entry.
  7. Position size = 1% account / (2×N).
  8. Once price rises 2×N, move stop to breakeven.
  9. Trail stop using 2×N from highest high (or new box floor).
  10. If pyramiding, add at 0.5N intervals (each with own stop).
  11. Stay with the trend until stop hits.
  12. Repeat.

Chapter 19 – Common Beginner Mistakes

  • Skipping the 1% rule.
  • Trading illiquid stocks.
  • Not using stops.

Appendix – Glossary & Key Formulas

ATR – Average True Range, a measure of volatility.
Box – consolidation area defined by support and resistance.
Breakout – price closes above box top.
N – Turtle term for ATR(20).
Trailing stop – stop that moves up with price.
1% rule – risk no more than 1% of account per trade.
Position size = (Account × 0.01) / (2 × N)

Formulas at a glance:
Stop distance = 2 × N
Pyramiding interval = 0.5 × N
