Introduction

Trading often appears mysterious. Many beginners believe success requires complex indicators, secret algorithms, or insider information.

In reality, some of the most successful trading systems are surprisingly simple. They are based on clear rules and disciplined execution.

Two of the most famous systems in trading history are:

  • Turtle Trading
  • Darvas Box Trading

Both systems rely on one central idea:

Markets tend to move in trends, and strong trends can continue longer than most people expect.

This book will teach you how to identify these trends and trade them systematically.

Chapter 1 — What Is Trading?

Trading means buying and selling financial assets such as stocks, commodities, currencies, or cryptocurrencies with the goal of making a profit.

Types of Financial Markets

Market Examples
Stock Market Apple, Microsoft, Nvidia
Commodities Gold, Oil, Wheat
Forex USD/EUR, GBP/JPY
Crypto Bitcoin, Ethereum

Although these markets are different, they share one common property: prices move based on supply and demand.

Kid Analogy

Imagine a toy store selling a popular toy. If many kids want the toy but only a few are available, the price goes up. If nobody wants the toy anymore, the price drops. Markets behave exactly the same way.

Chapter 2 — Understanding Market Trends

A trend describes the overall direction of price movement over time.

Trend Type Description
Uptrend Prices generally move higher
Downtrend Prices generally move lower
Sideways Prices move within a range

Trend Analogy

Imagine pushing a ball down a hill. Once it starts rolling, it usually keeps rolling. Trends in markets behave the same way.

Why Trends Exist

Markets trend because:
  • Investors react slowly to information
  • Large institutions accumulate positions over time
  • Momentum attracts additional buyers

Chapter 6 — Risk Management Fundamentals

Before learning specific strategies, every trader must understand one principle:

The goal of trading is not to win every trade. The goal is to survive long enough for winning trades to outweigh losing ones.

Professional traders think about risk before profit.

The 1% Rule

A widely used guideline is the 1% risk rule.

This means you never risk more than 1% of your trading capital on a single trade.

Account Size Maximum Risk per Trade
$5,000 $50
$10,000 $100
$50,000 $500
$100,000 $1,000

Why This Matters

Even great trading systems experience losing streaks.

Imagine flipping a coin. Even if the odds are fair, you can still get five tails in a row. Trading systems behave the same way.

Risk control ensures that a temporary losing streak does not destroy your capital.

Chapter 7 — The Darvas Box Strategy

The Darvas Box strategy is one of the simplest and most elegant breakout systems ever developed.

The core idea is based on a simple observation:

Stocks often move sideways for a period of time before breaking out into strong trends.

These sideways ranges form what we call price boxes.

How a Darvas Box Forms

A box forms when the price repeatedly hits a ceiling and a floor.

Level Meaning
Top of Box Resistance level
Bottom of Box Support level

As long as price remains within these boundaries, the stock is consolidating.

Think of a bouncing ball trapped inside a cardboard box. It keeps bouncing between the top and bottom. But if the ball suddenly jumps out of the box, something important has changed.

Chapter 8 — Identifying Darvas Boxes

To identify a Darvas Box, traders look for the following pattern:

  • A strong upward price movement
  • A pause where price moves sideways
  • Several attempts to break the high but failing

The repeated failure to break higher creates a clear resistance level.

Example Price Data

Day Price
1 45
2 48
3 49
4 50
5 49
6 48
7 50

Resistance level = 50 Support level = 48

This creates a Darvas Box between 48 and 50.

Chapter 9 — Breakouts

A breakout occurs when price moves above resistance or below support.

The Darvas strategy focuses on upward breakouts.

Why Breakouts Work

When price breaks resistance, it means buyers have overcome sellers.

Imagine a crowded stadium exit. People push against a closed door. Once the door opens, everyone rushes through. A breakout is similar — pent-up demand suddenly moves the price higher.

Darvas Entry Rule

Buy when price closes above the top of the Darvas box.

Chapter 10 — Stop Loss Placement

A stop loss defines the maximum acceptable loss.

Darvas Stop Rule

Chapter 14 — The Complete Turtle Trading Rules

Imagine driving a car at night. You cannot see the entire road ahead, but your headlights allow you to respond to what appears. Trend trading works the same way.

Chapter 16 — Exit Rules

Every trading system requires clear exit rules.

The Turtle system uses two types of exits:

  • Stop loss exit
  • Trend reversal exit

Stop Loss Exit

Stop Loss = Entry Price − (2 × N)

Where N represents market volatility.

Example

Entry Price = 100 N = 3 Stop Loss:
100 − (2 × 3) = 94

If the market drops to 94, the trade is closed automatically.

Chapter 17 — Position Sizing Explained

Position sizing determines how much capital is allocated to a trade.

Professional traders never invest randomly sized positions.

Instead, they calculate position size based on volatility and account size.

Simplified Position Formula

Position Size = Account Risk ÷ (2 × N)

Example Calculation

Account Size = $10,000 Risk per Trade = 1% Risk Amount:
$10,000 × 0.01 = $100
If: N = 2 Position Size:
$100 ÷ (2 × 2) = 25 shares

The trader buys 25 shares.

Chapter 32 — The Turtle Trader Risk Management System

Many beginner traders focus only on one thing:

Finding the perfect entry.

But professional traders know something very different.

Entries are important — but they are not the most important part of trading.

The most important part is risk management.

This is the secret that allowed the famous Turtle Traders to succeed.

The Turtle Trading experiment proved that ordinary people could become profitable traders if they followed strict rules for risk and position sizing.

The Famous Turtle Experiment

In the early 1980s, trader Richard Dennis made a bold claim.

He believed successful traders could be trained using simple rules.

His colleague William Eckhardt disagreed.

To settle the debate, they conducted a real-world experiment.

They recruited a group of beginners, trained them for two weeks, and gave them real money to trade.

These traders became known as the Turtle Traders.

The result shocked the financial world.

Many of the students became extremely successful traders.

Some later managed hundreds of millions of dollars.

Their secret was not prediction.

Their secret was discipline and risk control.

The Golden Rule of Trading

The Turtle Traders followed one rule above all others:

Never risk too much on a single trade.

Think of trading like playing a long tournament instead of a single game.

If you bet all your money on one round and lose, the tournament ends.

But if you risk only a small amount each time, you can continue playing until the big opportunity appears.

The Concept of “N” (Market Volatility)

The Turtle system measured market movement using a value called N.

N represents the average daily price movement of an asset.

This measurement tells traders how volatile the market is.

Think of volatility like waves in the ocean.

Some days the ocean is calm.

Other days the waves are huge.

Markets behave the same way.

Some stocks move slowly.

Others move dramatically.

Using N allows traders to adjust position sizes based on market conditions.

Simple Formula for N

A simplified beginner version is:

N = Average True Range (ATR) over 20 days

ATR measures the average daily movement of a stock.

Example:

  • Day 1 movement = $1.20
  • Day 2 movement = $1.50
  • Day 3 movement = $0.80

Average movement ≈ $1.20

So in this case:

N = $1.20

Setting the Stop Loss

The Turtle Traders placed their stop loss at:

2 × N

This distance allows the trade room to move naturally without being stopped too quickly.

Example:

  • N = $1.20
  • Stop distance = $2.40

If the entry price is $50, the stop loss becomes:

$50 − $2.40 = $47.60

Position Sizing

Position sizing determines how many shares to buy.

This is where most amateur traders make mistakes.

They choose position size based on emotion instead of mathematics.

The Turtle system used a simple rule.

Risk per trade should equal about 1% of the trading account.

Position Size Formula

The formula looks like this:

Position Size = Account Risk ÷ Stop Distance

Example:

  • Account size = $10,000
  • Risk per trade = 1%
  • Maximum risk = $100

If the stop distance is $2:

Position size = $100 ÷ $2

Position size = 50 shares

Why Small Risk Wins in the Long Run

Many beginner traders think risking small amounts is boring.

But small risk is what allows compounding to work.

Consider two traders.

Trader A risks 20% per trade.

Trader B risks 1% per trade.

Trader A only needs a few losing trades to lose most of their account.

Trader B can survive hundreds of trades.

Survival is the key to long-term success.

Pyramiding: Adding to Winning Trades

The Turtle Traders increased their positions when trades moved in their favor.

This process is called pyramiding.

Instead of betting big at the beginning, they added gradually as the trend strengthened.

Example:

  • Initial entry
  • Add position after 0.5N movement
  • Add another after another 0.5N

This allowed traders to build large positions in strong trends while keeping risk controlled.

Imagine planting seeds in a garden.

So you plant many seeds.

As some plants grow stronger, you give them more water and fertilizer.

The weak plants receive less attention.

Eventually the strongest plants dominate the garden.

This is exactly how trend traders manage positions.
