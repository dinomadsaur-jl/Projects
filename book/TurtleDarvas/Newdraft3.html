Part One – The Foundations of Trend Trading

Chapter 1 – What Is a Trend? The Physics of Markets

A trend is a persistent directional movement in price. But why do trends exist at all? Understanding this is crucial.

Markets trend because of three fundamental forces:

  • Information asymmetry: News and information spread slowly. Large institutions learn things first and trade accordingly. By the time the public hears, the trend is already underway.
  • Institutional accumulation: Big funds can't buy millions of shares instantly – they accumulate over weeks or months. This gradual buying pushes prices higher over time.
  • Behavioral herding: Humans are social animals. When we see prices rising, we want to join. This creates self-reinforcing momentum.
Physics analogy: A train leaving the station doesn't reach full speed instantly. It accelerates slowly, then maintains speed, then decelerates. Trends are the same – they start, persist, and eventually end. Your job is to ride during the persistent phase.

Trends exist in all timeframes and all markets. A 5-minute chart can trend. A weekly chart can trend. The principles are identical.

Chapter 2 – Why 90% of Beginners Fail

The statistics are brutal: studies show 80-95% of day traders lose money. But why? It's not because the market is random. It's because beginners make predictable mistakes:

  • No system: They trade based on feelings, news, or someone's tip.
  • No position sizing: They bet random amounts, often too large.
  • No stop losses: They hold losing trades hoping they'll come back.
  • Cutting winners short: They take quick profits but let losses run.
  • Revenge trading: After a loss, they double down to "get it back."

I once watched a beginner turn $50,000 into $2,000 in three months. He made one mistake repeatedly: he let losses run. A $500 loss became $2,000, then $5,000, because he couldn't admit he was wrong. The Turtles were taught to never do this.

The good news: every one of these mistakes is preventable with a rules-based system.

Chapter 3 – The Philosophy of Following, Not Predicting

Most beginners want to predict the future. They want to know what price will be next week. This is a trap.

Trend followers don't predict – they react. When price breaks out, they buy. When price reverses, they sell. They have no opinion about whether the market "should" go up or down. They simply follow what price does.

Surfer analogy: A surfer doesn't create waves. They don't predict exactly when a wave will arrive. They wait, they paddle when they see a wave forming, and they ride it as long as it lasts. If the wave collapses, they paddle out and wait for the next one.

This philosophy removes emotional burden. You don't need to be right about the future – you just need to react correctly to the present. Both the Turtles and Darvas embraced this mindset completely.

Chapter 7 – Understanding N: Volatility Decoded

At the heart of the Turtle system lies a concept called N. N measures volatility – how much a market typically moves in a day. [citation:3]

N is the 20-day exponential moving average of the True Range. For beginners, we can use the simpler 20-day Average True Range (ATR). [citation:7]

The True Range (TR) for any day is the greatest of:

  • Today's high minus today's low
  • Today's high minus yesterday's close (absolute value)
  • Today's low minus yesterday's close (absolute value)
TR = max(High - Low, |High - PrevClose|, |Low - PrevClose|)

Then N is calculated as:

N = (19 × Previous N + Current TR) / 20

This exponential formula gives more weight to recent volatility while maintaining stability. [citation:7]

Why N matters: N tells you the market's "average move." If N = $2 for a stock, it typically moves $2 per day. This allows you to:

  • Set stop distances proportional to market noise
  • Size positions so each trade has equal risk
  • Add positions at logical intervals
Real example – Apple stock (hypothetical):

Day 1: High 150, Low 148, PrevClose 149 → TR = max(2, 1, 1) = 2
Day 2: High 152, Low 149, PrevClose 150 → TR = max(3, 2, 1) = 3
Day 3: High 153, Low 151, PrevClose 151 → TR = max(2, 2, 0) = 2

If previous N = 2.0, then:
New N = (19 × 2.0 + 2.0) / 20 = (38 + 2)/20 = 40/20 = 2.0
So N remains 2.0, meaning average daily movement ≈ $2.

Chapter 8 – The Unit Formula with Full Factor Analysis

The Turtles didn't think in shares or contracts – they thought in Units. A Unit is a position sized so that 1 N represents 1% of account equity. [citation:3]

Unit = 1% of Account / (N × Dollars per Point)

For stocks, Dollars per Point = 1 (a $1 move = $1 profit/loss per share). For futures, this varies by contract.

Let's break this down with examples at different account sizes:

Why this works: When volatility (N) is high, you trade smaller positions. When volatility is low, you trade larger positions. This equalizes risk across different markets and times. [citation:7]

The Turtles could trade up to 4 Units in the same market (more on that in pyramiding). Each Unit was added at specific intervals.

Chapter 9 – Turtle Entry Rules: System 1 & System 2

The Turtles used two related but distinct entry systems: [citation:1]

System 1 (20-day breakout): Enter when price exceeds the highest high of the last 20 days. This is faster, more sensitive, but produces more false signals.

System 2 (55-day breakout): Enter when price exceeds the highest high of the last 55 days. This is slower, captures bigger trends, but has fewer signals.

Most Turtles traded both systems simultaneously, with half their capital allocated to each. The logic: System 1 catches early breakouts; System 2 confirms major trends.

Breakout example:

Stock XYZ has 20-day high of $50. Today it closes at $51. That's a System 1 buy signal. You enter tomorrow at market.

If it also breaks a 55-day high of $52, that's a System 2 signal – even stronger confirmation.

Chapter 10 – Turtle Exit Rules: The 2N Stop

Exits are where the Turtle system truly shines. The initial stop is set at 2 × N below entry. [citation:5][citation:7]

Initial Stop = Entry Price − (2 × N)

Why 2N? This distance is wide enough that normal daily noise won't shake you out, but tight enough that losses are controlled to about 2% of account (since Unit size was based on 1% risk per N).

Example: Enter at $50, N = $2 → Initial stop at $46. Maximum loss = $4 per share. If Unit size was 100 shares (from earlier example), max loss = $400, or 4% of $10,000 account. But remember, 1 Unit should risk 1% – here we risk 2%? Let's check the math carefully.

Actually, the Turtles defined Unit so that 1N move = 1% of account. So a 2N stop = 2% risk per Unit. They limited total portfolio risk across multiple Units.

For beginners, I recommend a simpler approach: risk 1% of account total, and set stop at 2N. Then position size = (1% account) / (2 × N). This matches our earlier formula and keeps risk exactly 1% per trade.

Chapter 11 – Pyramiding: Adding to Winners

The Turtles didn't just buy once and hold. They built positions gradually as trends developed. This is called pyramiding. [citation:1]

The rule: Add another Unit every 0.5N move in your direction. [citation:7]

Example with N = $4, entry at $100:

  • Initial Unit at $100
  • Add 2nd Unit at $102 (100 + 0.5×4)
  • Add 3rd Unit at $104 (100 + 1.0×4)
  • Add 4th Unit at $106 (100 + 1.5×4)

Maximum position size was 4 Units in any single market. Each Unit had its own stop at 2N below its entry price. So the earliest Units had the widest stops, later Units had tighter stops.

Pyramiding in action:

Unit 1 (100 shares) at $100, stop at $92
Unit 2 (100 shares) at $102, stop at $94
Unit 3 (100 shares) at $104, stop at $96
Unit 4 (100 shares) at $106, stop at $98

If price reverses from $110 down to $98, Unit 4 stops out. If it continues down, more Units stop. The profit from early Units protects later ones.

This technique allows you to have your largest position when the trend is strongest, while keeping risk controlled.

Part Three – The Complete Darvas Box System

Chapter 12 – Nicolas Darvas: The Dancer Who Beat Wall Street

Budapest, 1920s. Nicolas Darvas was born into a world far from finance. He studied economics at the University of Budapest, but his life took a dramatic turn when Soviet and Nazi forces converged on Hungary. He fled, eventually immigrating to the United States with his sister. [citation:2]

In America, Darvas and his sister formed a dance team that became internationally famous. They performed in the finest hotels across the world – the St. Regis in New York, the Savoy in London, the George V in Paris. But while dancing, Darvas's mind was elsewhere. [citation:2]

He became obsessed with the stock market. He read everything he could find. He started with small investments, lost money, and kept learning. The brokers of the 1950s pushed dividend-paying blue chips, but Darvas noticed something odd: some stocks would move sideways for weeks, then explode upward on heavy volume. [citation:2]

He began tracking these patterns while traveling. With no internet, no mobile phones, he relied on telegrams from his broker. Each week, he'd receive price updates and draw boxes on graph paper in his hotel room. The boxes represented consolidation ranges. When prices broke out of a box with volume, he bought. [citation:6][citation:10]

Over 18 months, Darvas turned an initial investment of $10,000 into $2.25 million. He later wrote "How I Made $2,000,000 in the Stock Market," which became an instant classic. [citation:2][citation:10]

Chapter 13 – From $10,000 to $2 Million in 18 Months

Let's put that return in perspective: $10,000 to $2.25 million is a 22,400% return in 1.5 years. Even allowing for 1950s market conditions, this is extraordinary. [citation:2]

Darvas achieved this while dancing full-time. He wasn't sitting at a screen all day – he received price updates weekly and made decisions from hotel rooms. His success came not from speed, but from discipline.

His biggest winners included Texas Instruments, Zenith Radio, and Thiokol Chemical – all growth stocks of the era. He rode them up, added as new boxes formed, and used trailing stops to protect profits.

Chapter 14 – Darvas Box Theory: The Original Method

The Darvas Box is both simple and elegant. A box forms when a stock trades within a defined price range for several days or weeks. [citation:2][citation:10]

  • Box top (ceiling): The highest price reached during the consolidation
  • Box bottom (floor): The lowest price during the consolidation

For a valid box, Darvas looked for at least three touches of the top without breaking through. This showed resistance was genuine.

Box formation example:

Stock rises to $50, pulls back to $48, rises to $50 again, pulls back to $49, rises to $50. That's three touches of $50. Box top = $50, bottom = $48 (lowest pullback).

Chapter 15 – How Darvas Identified Boxes with Telegrams

This part is fascinating: Darvas didn't have real-time charts. He received telegrams with weekly price ranges. He'd plot them on graph paper and look for patterns. [citation:6][citation:10]

His method required patience. A box might take weeks to form. But that patience paid off – he only traded the strongest setups.

He also kept detailed trading journals, recording why he entered each trade, where his stop was, and what he learned. This practice of journaling is one of his most underrated contributions. [citation:6]

Chapter 16 – Volume Confirmation: Darvas' Secret Weapon

Darvas noticed something critical: breakouts without volume often failed. Breakouts with surging volume usually succeeded. [citation:2][citation:6][citation:10]

He looked for volume at least 50% above the 10-day average on breakout days. This showed institutional accumulation – big players buying in size.

Volume Confirmation = Breakout Day Volume > 1.5 × 10-Day Average Volume

Modern traders can easily scan for this. In the 1950s, Darvas calculated it manually from newspaper tables.

Chapter 17 – Darvas Entry and Exit Rules

Darvas's entry rules were precise: [citation:6]

  • Buy signal: Price closes above the box top
  • Entry price: About 1-2% above box top, to confirm the breakout is real
  • Initial stop: Just below the box bottom (often the low of the box)
  • Exit if: Price falls back into the old box – this signals failure
If stopped out but price re-enters the higher box, Darvas would buy again. He didn't hold grudges against stocks – if the signal reappeared, he re-entered. [citation:6]

Chapter 18 – Trailing Stops the Darvas Way

As a stock rose, new boxes formed at higher levels. Darvas would raise his stop to just below each new box floor. [citation:2][citation:6][citation:10]

Example progression:

  • Box 1: $45-$50, buy at $51, stop at $44
  • Box 2: $52-$58, raise stop to $51
  • Box 3: $60-$68, raise stop to $59
  • Box 4: $70-$80, raise stop to $69

If price reversed from $85 down to $68, the stop at $69 would exit with substantial profit locked in.

This is the essence of "letting winners run" – you keep raising the safety net, never lowering it.

Part Four – The Hybrid System: Turtle-Darvas Combined

The Turtle system provides superior risk management and position sizing. The Darvas system provides precise entry points and intuitive box formations. Together, they form a complete methodology.

Think of it this way:

  • Darvas tells you WHEN to enter – at box breakouts with volume
  • Turtle tells you HOW MUCH to buy – using N-based position sizing
  • Both tell you WHEN to exit – using trailing stops (Turtle's 2N or Darvas box floors)

Chapter 20 – Complete Hybrid Ruleset

Here is the full, integrated system:

Market Selection:

  • Liquid stocks or ETFs on major exchanges
  • Minimum average daily volume > 1 million shares
  • Price > $10 (to avoid penny stock manipulation)

Trend Filter (optional but recommended):

  • Price above 200-day moving average (long-term uptrend)
  • Or 50-day above 200-day (golden cross condition)

Box Identification:

  • Look for consolidation of at least 10-20 days
  • At least three touches of resistance without breaking
  • Clear support level from pullbacks
  • Box top = highest high in consolidation
  • Box bottom = lowest low in consolidation

Entry Signal:

  • Price closes above box top
  • Volume > 1.5 × 20-day average volume (confirmation)
  • Buy next day at market, or 1-2% above box top

Volatility Calculation:

  • N = 20-day Average True Range (ATR)
  • Calculate daily using high-low range relative to previous close

Position Sizing:

  • Risk per trade = 1% of total account
  • Stop distance = 2 × N
  • Position size (shares) = (Account × 0.01) / (2 × N)
  • Round down to nearest whole share

Initial Stop:

  • Place stop at: Entry Price − (2 × N)
  • Also consider box bottom – use whichever is tighter initially

Pyramiding (optional):

  • Add another Unit when price moves 0.5N above entry
  • Maximum 4 Units total
  • Each Unit sized using same formula based on current account
  • Each Unit has its own stop at 2N below its entry

Trailing Stop – Let Winners Run:

  • Method A (Turtle): Stop = Highest Price Since Entry − (2 × N)
  • Method B (Darvas): Stop = Floor of most recent Darvas box (lowest price of new box)
  • Method C (Hybrid): Use whichever is higher – gives tightest protection

Exit Rules:

  • Stop loss hit → exit entire position
  • New box breaks down (price closes below box floor) → exit
  • No exit based on time – only price

Chapter 22 – Position Sizing with N and Account Risk

Let's dive deeper into the factor applied to N. The standard formula is:

Position Size = (Account × Risk%) / (Factor × N)

Where Factor is typically 2 (for a 2N stop). But you can adjust factor based on your risk tolerance:

Larger factor = wider stops = smaller positions = lower risk per trade. Beginners should start with factor 2 and adjust only after experience.

Chapter 23 – Letting Winners Run: Advanced Trailing Stops

This is the most important skill to develop. Let's explore trailing stop methods in depth:

Fixed-Percentage Trail: Simple but flawed – doesn't adapt to volatility. A 10% trail might be too tight in volatile stocks.

ATR-Based Trail (Turtle): Trail = Highest Price − (2 × N). This expands and contracts with market volatility.

Parabolic SAR: An indicator that accelerates as trend continues. Too complex for beginners.

Moving Average Trail: Exit when price closes below 20-day or 50-day MA. Slower but captures big trends.

Box Trail (Darvas): Raise stop to floor of each new box. Combines price action with volatility.

For our hybrid system, I recommend the Hybrid Trail: Use the higher of (Highest Price − 2N) and (Most Recent Box Floor). This gives you the best of both worlds.

  6. N calculation: 20-day ATR, recalculated weekly
  7. Position size formula: (Account × 0.01) / (2 × N)
  8. Initial stop: 2N below entry, or box bottom if tighter
  9. Trailing stop method: Highest price − 2N OR new box floor
  10. Pyramiding rules: Yes/No, if yes, intervals and max units
  11. Maximum positions: Never exceed 6-8 simultaneous trades
  12. Journal requirements: Every trade logged with entry, exit, reason, lessons

Chapter 29 – Common Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

  • Mistake 1: Moving stops down. Never lower a stop – if you lose confidence, exit entirely and re-evaluate.
  • Mistake 2: Taking profits too early. The big money is made in the 20% of trades that run. Let them run.
  • Mistake 3: Ignoring volume. Breakouts without volume are traps.
  • Mistake 4: Over-trading. Wait for clear boxes. Patience is a skill.
  • Mistake 5: Changing rules during drawdowns. The system works over many trades – not each trade.
  • Mistake 6: Position sizes too large. If you're losing sleep, you're risking too much.
  • Mistake 7: Not journaling. Writing forces clarity and provides data for improvement.

Appendix – Complete Formulas, Glossary, Resources

Key Formulas

True Range (TR): max(High - Low, |High - PrevClose|, |Low - PrevClose|)
N (20-day ATR): (19×PrevN + CurrentTR) / 20
Position Size: (Account × 0.01) / (2 × N)
Initial Stop: Entry Price − (2 × N)
Pyramiding Interval: 0.5 × N
Trailing Stop: Highest Price Since Entry − (2 × N)
Volume Confirmation: Breakout Volume > 1.5 × 20-day Avg Volume

Glossary

  • ATR (Average True Range): A measure of volatility developed by Welles Wilder, similar to Turtle N.
  • Box (Darvas): A price consolidation range with defined support and resistance levels.
  • Breakout: Price movement above resistance or below support, often signaling trend start.
  • Drawdown: Peak-to-trough decline in account value during a trading period.
  • N: Turtle term for 20-day exponential moving average of True Range.
  • Profit Factor: Gross profit divided by gross loss – should be > 1.5 for viable systems.
  • Pyramiding: Adding to winning positions as trend progresses.
  • Stop Loss: Predetermined price at which you exit a losing trade.
  • Trailing Stop: Stop that moves with price to lock in profits.
  • Unit: Turtle term for a standardized position sized by N.
